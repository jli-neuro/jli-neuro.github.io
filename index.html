About Me

My research uses computational models to understand how intelligent agents—both human and artificial—measure their own capabilities. For instance, how do we distinguish "what we can do" from "how sure we are"? How do miscalibrated beliefs about our own competence lead to maladaptive behaviors like mania? And can we build AI agents that know when to abstain from answering?

I am currently a PhD Candidate in Neuroscience at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai, advised by Angela Radulescu. I am also a Research Affiliate with Mark Ho at NYU. My work integrates Reinforcement Learning and Bayesian Inference to bridge clinical phenomenology with AI alignment.

Prior to my PhD, I was a Research Assistant at Brigham and Women's Hospital in Boston, where I built computational pipelines for lesion network analysis. I hold a B.S. in Neuroscience from Duke University.

Selected Research Interests

Computational Psychiatry: Modeling self-efficacy as a dynamic belief state; understanding mania as an attractor state in belief updating; mapping clinical instability to robust-learning failure modes.

AI Alignment & Cognitive Modeling: Calibration metrics for LLMs; offline-to-online reinforcement learning; studying OOD generalization and hallucination reduction through the lens of metacognition.
