About Me

I study how people learn what they can and cannot do—and how those beliefs shape motivation, planning, and reward learning. My work connects ideas from computational psychiatry and cognitive science (e.g., self-efficacy, belief updating, generalization) with reinforcement learning and Bayesian modeling. Broadly, I’m interested in how agents calibrate their capability under uncertainty, and how miscalibration can lead to maladaptive decision-making.

I’m a PhD candidate in Neuroscience at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai (New York, NY), working with Dr. Angela Radulescu. I’m also affiliated with NYU through collaborations with Professor Mark Ho. I build behavioral tasks and computational models to measure and explain individual differences in reward generalization and capability beliefs, with a focus on mechanisms relevant to mania risk and related constructs.

Prior to my PhD, I was a Research Assistant at Brigham and Women’s Hospital in Boston, where I built computational pipelines for lesion network analysis. I hold a B.S. in Neuroscience from Duke University.

Keywords: computational psychiatry · reinforcement learning · Bayesian cognitive modeling · self-efficacy / capability calibration · reward generalization · planning under uncertainty

Research Themes

  • Capability beliefs and calibration: How do agents infer their ability from noisy outcomes? How do “can-do” beliefs guide goal selection, persistence, and exploration?
  • Reward generalization and value propagation: When do people over-generalize from positive outcomes? How can value propagate too far in sequential decision problems—and what does that imply for mood-related risk?
  • Tasks as behavioral assays: Designing paradigms that produce identifiable model signatures (not just effects), with stability for individual-differences and translational use.

Current Projects

  • Behavioral assay of positive overgeneralization: A task framework that quantifies reward generalization in learning, linked to self-reported positive overgeneralization and motivational traits.
  • Dynamic self-efficacy / capability calibration models: Models where capability beliefs interact with value learning (e.g., weighting high-value outcomes) to shape planning and generalization.
  • Motor planning under internal uncertainty: How internal motor noise and action uncertainty should be represented in planning and decision-making.
