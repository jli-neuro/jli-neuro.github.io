About Me

I study how people learn what they can and cannot do—and how those self-efficacy beliefs shape motivation, decision-making planning. My work connects ideas from computational psychiatry and cognitive science (e.g., self-efficacy, belief updating, generalization) with reinforcement learning and Bayesian modeling. Broadly, I’m interested in how agents calibrate their capability under uncertainty, and how miscalibration can lead to maladaptive decision-making.

I’m a PhD candidate in Neuroscience at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai (New York, NY), working with Dr. Angela Radulescu. I’m also affiliated with NYU through collaborations with Professor Mark Ho. I build behavioral tasks and computational models to measure and explain individual differences in reward generalization and self-efficacy beliefs, with a focus on mechanisms relevant to mania risk and related constructs.

Prior to my PhD, I was a Research Assistant at Brigham and Women’s Hospital in Boston, where I built computational pipelines for lesion network analysis. I hold a B.S. in Neuroscience from Duke University.

Keywords: computational psychiatry · reinforcement learning · Bayesian cognitive modeling · self-efficacy / capability calibration · reward generalization · planning under uncertainty
